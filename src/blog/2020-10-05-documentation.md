Documentation is the bane of my existence.

There. I said it. And I work in data, where half my job is technically *about* creating a record of things.

The irony is not lost on me.

But here's the other thing I've had to admit: documentation has saved my life more than once. An email I needed to refer back to months later. A set of steps I wrote down at 11pm that got me out of a jam the following Tuesday. A process I documented mostly for myself that ended up onboarding three new team members.

So I have a complicated relationship with it. I avoid it, then I'm grateful for it. I skip it, then I pay for it.

## The real problem with documentation

The question was never *whether* to document. That argument is settled. The question was always *how* and *where*.

Because if the system is annoying, you won't use it. If it's hard to find, it might as well not exist. If it requires a lot of formatting work, you'll always find a reason to do it later — which means never.

I needed something that was low friction to write and easy to find when I needed it. One less excuse to put it off.

## What actually changed for me

I started writing in Markdown. Plain text, simple formatting rules, works everywhere. No special software. No fighting with a toolbar. Just write.

That small shift made documentation feel less like overhead and more like thinking out loud. The barrier dropped enough that I actually started doing it.

The other shift was deciding that a short, imperfect record is worth infinitely more than a perfect one that doesn't exist. I stopped waiting until I had time to write something thorough. Notes work. Bullet points work. A two-sentence summary of a decision works.

> Done and findable beats perfect and missing.

## The habit I'm still building

I won't pretend I've solved this. Some weeks are better than others. I still catch myself making a fix or a decision and moving on without writing it down — then regretting it two months later.

But the threshold has shifted. I document more than I used to, and it costs me less effort than it used to. That feels like progress.

If you're in the same boat — you know it matters, you just can't make it stick — start smaller than you think you need to. One sentence is enough. A date and a decision is enough. Build the habit before you build the system.

The system can come later. The habit is the hard part.
