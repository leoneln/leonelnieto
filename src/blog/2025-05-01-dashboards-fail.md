After years of building dashboards for executives, directors, and frontline managers, I've noticed a pattern: most dashboards fail not because the data is wrong, but because they are built to *show* rather than to *teach, guide, and decide*.

That distinction matters.

Data visualization is both art and science. The science helps us understand how people process visual information, how memory works, and how easily we overload an audience. The art helps us create focus, structure, and meaning. When those two come together, a dashboard becomes more than a collection of charts. It becomes a tool for understanding.

Most dashboards do not get there.

## The Display Trap

A display dashboard answers the question: *What happened?*

A decision dashboard answers: *What should we do about it?*

Most organizations build the first kind and wonder why nobody uses it.

The difference is not technical. It is about intent. When a data team builds a dashboard, we often default to giving stakeholders everything: all the metrics, all the filters, all the breakdowns, and all the possible ways to slice the data.

The assumption is that more information leads to better decisions.

It does not.

More information often creates more work for the audience. It increases cognitive load. It forces people to search, compare, remember, and interpret without enough guidance. A dashboard may be technically accurate and still fail because the viewer cannot quickly understand what matters.

Information without context is just noise.

Executives do not need 47 charts. They need the few things that matter right now, clearly framed, visually prioritized, and connected to a decision they actually need to make.

## The Brain Is the Real User

Good dashboards are not just designed for screens. They are designed for people.

People do not look at a dashboard as neutral machines. They scan. They notice contrast. They respond to color. They rely on prior experience. They use fast, intuitive thinking first, then slower analytical thinking when the design asks them to do more work.

That means every visual choice matters.

Color can guide attention, or it can create distraction. A label can clarify, or it can add clutter. A filter can support exploration, or it can overwhelm the user. A chart can invite understanding, or it can force the audience to decode what should have been obvious.

This is where cognitive load becomes practical. A dashboard should:

- Manage the complexity of the data.
- Remove unnecessary visual noise.
- Help the viewer focus on the meaning.

That is why simple visuals often work best. A bar chart is not boring if it helps people compare quickly. A line chart is not basic if it makes a trend clear. White space is not wasted space if it gives the audience room to think.

The goal is not to impress people with the complexity of the dashboard. The goal is to reduce the amount of effort required to understand the point.

## Form Follows Function

One of the most useful design principles for dashboards is simple: form follows function.

Start with the purpose of the dashboard, then design the visual form to support that purpose.

If the purpose is to compare categories, use a chart that makes comparison easy. If the purpose is to show change over time, use a chart that helps the eye follow a trend. If the purpose is to focus attention on one issue, use visual hierarchy, annotations, and selective color to make that issue stand out.

Do not start with the tool. Start with the task.

Power BI, Tableau, R, Python, and other tools can all produce good visuals. They can also produce cluttered, confusing, and misleading visuals. The tool is not the strategy. The design choices are the strategy.

A useful dashboard usually has a clear answer to three questions:

**Who is this for?**  
Not a generic audience, but a real person or group with a real need.

**What are they trying to understand?**  
Not just what data is available, but what issue, pattern, risk, or opportunity needs attention.

**How should the dashboard help them act?**  
Not just how they interact with the dashboard, but how the dashboard supports the next conversation, decision, or intervention.

When those questions are unclear, the dashboard becomes a storage place for charts. When those questions are clear, the dashboard becomes a decision tool.

## From Charts to Story

A dashboard should not be a random collection of facts. It should have a flow.

That does not mean every dashboard needs to be dramatic. It means the information should be arranged in a way that helps the audience move from context to insight to action. I like the Data Storytelling framework from Duarte [1](https://www.effectivedatastorytelling.com/post/data-storytelling-demystifying-narrative-structure-in-data-stories).

A strong data story usually has four parts:

**Setting and hook:** What are we looking at, and why does it matter now?

**Raising points:** What patterns, comparisons, or changes help us understand the issue?

**Aha moment:** What is the main insight or key finding?

**Solution and next steps:** What should we do next?

This structure matters because charts alone do not create meaning. A beautiful visual without a narrative can still fail. The audience needs help understanding what they are seeing, why it matters, and what to do with it.

That is the real work of data storytelling.

## What I Ask Before Building Anything

Before I build a dashboard, I try to slow the process down and ask better questions.

**Who is the audience?**  
A dashboard for an executive meeting should not look like a dashboard for an analyst doing root-cause analysis.

**What decision does this support?**  
If we cannot name the decision, we may be building a report instead of a tool.

**What does “good” look like?**  
A number without context is incomplete. Is it above target? Below target? Improving? Declining? Stable? Unusual?

**Where should the audience look first?**  
If everything is emphasized, nothing is emphasized.

**What action should this make easier?**  
A dashboard should reduce friction between insight and action.

These questions are simple, but they change the work. They move the conversation away from “What charts do you want?” and toward “What understanding do we need to create?”

## A Practical Fix

Before your next dashboard build, write one sentence:

*This dashboard will help [specific person or group] understand [specific issue] so they can decide [specific action] on [specific cadence].*

For example:

*This dashboard will help enrollment leaders understand weekly registration patterns so they can decide where outreach, advising, or section adjustments are needed before the start of the term.*

That sentence does a lot of work. It names the audience. It defines the purpose. It connects the dashboard to a decision. It also creates a standard for what should and should not be included.

If a chart does not support that sentence, it probably does not belong.

## Design Is Also an Ethical Choice

Dashboards can persuade. That means we have a responsibility to use them carefully.

We should not use design to hide weak data, exaggerate a trend, or push a conclusion the evidence does not support. We should use design to clarify, not distort. We should provide enough context for people to understand the limits of the data. We should avoid visual tricks that make small differences look dramatic or make uncertainty disappear.

A good dashboard is not just clear. It is trustworthy.

That trust is built through honest scales, clear labels, thoughtful color, accessible design, and a willingness to show the audience what the data can and cannot say.

## What To Do Instead

Do not build dashboards that simply display data.

Build dashboards that help people see what matters.

Build dashboards that respect how people think.

Build dashboards that reduce clutter, guide attention, and make the “so what?” clear.

Build dashboards that combine the science of perception with the art of communication.

Most importantly, build dashboards for real people making real decisions in real moments.

> The goal is not a beautiful dashboard. The goal is a better decision made by a real person with a clearer understanding of what the data means.

That reframe changes everything.
