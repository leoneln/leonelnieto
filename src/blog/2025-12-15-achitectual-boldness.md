## The Real Reason Organizations Move Slowly

Most organizations do not move slowly because people are lazy, careless, or resistant for no reason. They move slowly because the architecture of the organization makes speed hard.

By architecture, I do not mean the building. I mean the way decisions move, the way teams are organized, the way problems are defined, the way power is distributed, and the way people are allowed—or not allowed—to act.

I have been thinking about this idea as **architectural boldness**: the discipline of redesigning how an organization works so that people can move faster, solve real problems, and act with more confidence.

Many of these principles came into sharper focus for me through a Strategic Leadership course at the University of Utah David Eccles School of Business, which I highly recommend. The course is connected to the Goff Strategic Leadership Institute, whose approach emphasizes real-world challenges, ambiguity, and challenging the status quo. The [Eccles Executive Education](https://eccles.utah.edu/programs/executive-education/product/mastering-strategic-leadership/) listing describes strategic leaders as people who identify resources, drive growth, and accept appropriate risk.

That last phrase matters: **appropriate risk**. Architectural boldness is not recklessness. It is the choice to stop confusing caution with wisdom.

## The Organization Has Antibodies

Large organizations often behave like living systems. When something new enters the body, the system reacts. It protects itself.

That is useful when the threat is real. But it becomes a problem when the “threat” is a better process, a clearer strategy, a faster decision, or a needed change. These are the organization’s antibodies.

They show up as:

* “We tried that before.”
* “We need more input.”
* “Let’s form a committee.”
* “This is not the right time.”
* “We should wait until everything is clear.”

Sometimes those concerns are valid. Often, they are the language of delay. This is a core trap of complex organizations: small, cautious changes are often absorbed or resisted by the system, while bold changes have a better chance of breaking through institutional inertia. 

> Small changes are easy for a system to ignore. Bold changes force the system to respond.

## The Problem With Trying to Please Everyone

One of the hardest leadership lessons is that moving fast often means disappointing someone. That does not mean being careless with people. It means being honest about tradeoffs.

When leaders try to satisfy every constituency, they often end up with solutions that are too slow, too vague, or too watered down to matter. The organization gets the appearance of alignment without the benefit of movement.

This is one of the quiet dangers of consensus culture. Consensus can build trust, but it can also become a hiding place for indecision.

Architectural boldness asks a different question:

**What is the smallest group of people needed to make a high-quality decision, and what structure will let them act quickly?**

That question changes everything.

## The Curse of Knowledge Makes Strategy Sound Like Noise

One of my favorite leadership concepts is the **curse of knowledge**. The idea is simple: once you know something deeply, it becomes hard to remember what it felt like not to know it.

Chip Heath and Dan Heath explain that many strategies fail because executives speak in broad, abstract language. Leaders may have concrete meaning in their heads, but employees often hear only vague phrases that do not translate into action. ([Harvard Business Review](https://hbr.org/2006/12/the-curse-of-knowledge))

This is why strategy often sounds clear in the leadership room and confusing everywhere else. Leaders hear the melody. Everyone else hears taps on the table.

Architectural boldness requires leaders to translate strategy into simple, concrete, usable language:

* What are we changing?
* Why does it matter?
* What will be different for people?
* What decision do we need now?
* What are we going to stop doing?

If people cannot repeat the strategy in plain language, they probably cannot act on it.

## Do Not Solve the Problem Under the Streetlight

Another trap is the **streetlight effect**: looking for answers where the light is good instead of where the real problem is.

In organizations, this often means leaders chase the data that is easiest to measure, the process that is easiest to see, or the symptom that is easiest to discuss.

But the visible problem is not always the real problem.

A team may think it has a reporting problem when it actually has a decision-rights problem.

A company may think it has a technology problem when it actually has a process problem.

A department may think it has a staffing problem when it actually has a prioritization problem.

Dwayne Spradlin’s Harvard Business Review article, “Are You Solving the Right Problem?”, makes this point directly: many organizations undermine their innovation efforts because they do not define the problem well enough before moving to solutions. ([Harvard Business Review](https://hbr.org/2012/09/are-you-solving-the-right-problem))

Architectural boldness begins with better problem formulation.

Before asking, “What should we do?” leaders should ask:

**What problem are we actually solving, and what makes this problem hard to solve in our current structure?**

## Speed Requires Structure

Speed does not happen by telling people to move faster. Speed happens when the organization is designed for movement.

That means fewer unnecessary approvals. Clearer ownership. Better decision rules. Stronger trust. Shorter feedback loops. More direct communication. More room for frontline expertise.

One of the best-known examples is Jack Welch’s push for a **boundaryless organization** at GE. Harvard Business Review describes how Welch used GE’s Work-Out process to bring people together across levels, functions, and geographies to solve problems and make decisions in real time. ([Harvard Business Review](https://hbr.org/2015/09/jack-welchs-approach-to-breaking-down-silos-still-works))

The lesson is not that every organization should copy GE. The lesson is that structure shapes behavior.

If you want collaboration, you cannot reward silos.

If you want speed, you cannot require every decision to climb five levels.

If you want ownership, you cannot keep all authority at the top.

If you want innovation, you cannot punish every failed experiment.

## Boldness Is Not the Opposite of Discipline

Some people hear “boldness” and assume it means moving without evidence.

That is not what I mean. Boldness without discipline is chaos.

Discipline without boldness is bureaucracy.

The goal is both.

Architectural boldness means making stronger choices about where the organization needs to move quickly, where it needs to slow down, and where it needs to stop pretending that more process will create more clarity.

This requires a different leadership rhythm:

1. Define the real problem.
2. Name the tradeoffs.
3. Put the right people in the room.
4. Make the decision.
5. Test quickly.
6. Learn publicly.
7. Adjust without shame.

That last point matters. Bold organizations do not need to be right all the time. They need to learn faster than they protect themselves.

## Values Beat Scorecards

Metrics matter. I believe deeply in measurement. I work with data and analytics every day.

But metrics alone do not create culture.

In fact, when used poorly, metrics can create sandbagging, gaming, fear, and narrow thinking.

Values shape how people behave when the scorecard is not watching.

If an organization wants to move faster, it needs values that support speed:

* Tell the truth early.
* Use plain language.
* Ask better questions.
* Make decisions close to the work.
* Treat learning as progress.
* Fix the system, not just the symptom.
* Focus on the mission, not the meeting.

A scorecard can tell you whether you are moving.

Values determine whether people are brave enough to move.

## Focus Is a Leadership Act

One of the simplest but hardest parts of leadership is focus.

Most organizations do not suffer from a lack of priorities. They suffer from too many priorities.

Everything is important, so nothing is first.

Architectural boldness requires ruthless prioritization. Not because other things do not matter, but because organizations have limited attention, trust, time, and energy.

A leader’s job is not to keep adding to the list.

A leader’s job is to help the organization know what matters most right now. Practice focusing sequentially: move the organization through one important challenge before diffusing attention across too many efforts. 

That is not easy. But it is honest.

## What Architectural Boldness Looks Like in Practice

Architectural boldness can show up in many ways:

* Replacing a standing committee with a short-term decision sprint.
* Giving frontline teams authority to test fixes without waiting months for approval.
* Creating cross-functional teams around problems instead of departments.
* Stopping low-value work to create capacity for higher-value work.
* Simplifying strategy into language people can actually use.
* Making decisions with 90% confidence instead of waiting forever for 100%.
* Naming the real tradeoff instead of hiding behind vague agreement.

These moves may sound simple. They are not.

They challenge habits, status, comfort, and control.

That is why they are architectural.

They do not just change what the organization does. They change how the organization is allowed to work.

## The Leadership Question

Every organization has pain points. Every organization has meetings about those pain points. Every organization has people who know what needs to change.

The question is not whether the organization has ideas.

The question is whether the organization has the architecture to act on them.

Architectural boldness asks leaders to stop admiring complexity and start redesigning the conditions for progress.

Because in the end, slow organizations are not always slow by accident.

Sometimes they are slow by design.

And if they are slow by design, they can be redesigned.
