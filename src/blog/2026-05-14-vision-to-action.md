## Vision Is Not Enough

Colleges are very good at naming bold aspirations. We write strategic plans, define student success goals, build dashboards, and align language around mission. But vision alone does not change institutional behavior.

The harder work is translation.

How does a broad goal become a coordinated set of actions? How do separate departments move together? How does evidence inform decisions before it is too late to adjust? How does planning connect to budgeting, assessment, and accountability?

That is where institutional effectiveness becomes more than reporting. It becomes architecture.

> The challenge is not whether the college has a vision. The challenge is whether the college has built the capacity to act on it.

## The Vision Matrix as Operating Architecture

The Vision Matrix was designed to close the gap between aspiration and implementation. Its power was not simply in naming outcomes, but in creating a structure that connected:

- Institutional goals
- Strategy teams
- Implementation actions
- Leading indicators
- Intermediate outcomes
- Final outcomes
- Regular check-ins
- Cross-functional accountability

In other words, the Matrix made the work visible.

It helped the college move from “Here is what we hope to accomplish” to “Here is what we are doing, who owns it, how we will know whether it is working, and when we will adjust.”

That distinction matters. Many institutions confuse activity with impact. A meeting is not an outcome. A dashboard is not a strategy. A pilot is not institutional change unless it is connected to a learning cycle, a decision point, and a sustainability plan.

## Architectural Boldness in Higher Education

Architectural boldness is not about being reckless. It is not about bypassing shared governance or moving fast for the sake of speed.

In higher education, architectural boldness means redesigning the conditions under which institutional work happens.

It means asking harder questions:

- Are we solving the real problem or the easiest problem to measure?
- Are we optimizing for perfect plans or timely learning?
- Are our teams empowered, or are they carrying responsibility without authority?
- Are we breaking silos, or simply inviting people from silos into the same meeting?
- Are our metrics supporting improvement, or merely documenting compliance?

This is where institutional effectiveness plays a critical role. The IE function can help the college avoid the “streetlight effect” — measuring what is easiest to see instead of what most needs to change.

## What Worked

Several design choices helped the Vision Matrix gain traction.

First, the goals were simple enough to remember. The college organized its work around three clear student-centered outcomes: engaging more students, helping more students complete, and improving students’ post-completion return on investment.

Second, the results-chain model helped teams connect action to impact. Teams had to articulate implementation actions, early outcomes, intermediate outcomes, and final institutional goals.

Third, the 30-30 cadence created movement. Rather than waiting for perfect plans, teams identified short-term actions, reviewed progress, surfaced barriers, and adjusted.

Fourth, the structure created cross-functional visibility. Teams began to see overlaps, dependencies, and duplication that were previously hidden.

That visibility was one of the most important outcomes of the process.

## What Bent Under Pressure

The work also revealed stress points.

Some barriers were predictable: budget uncertainty, faculty availability, data access, role confusion, and competing priorities. Others were cultural: resistance to new work, unclear ownership, and the persistence of silos.

These were not side issues. They were architectural issues.

A strategy that depends on informal ownership will struggle when staff leave or priorities shift. A project that depends on inaccessible data will struggle to improve in real time. A cross-functional initiative without clear decision rights will generate conversation, but not always action.

That is why bold institutional change requires more than enthusiasm. It requires ownership, authority, cadence, evidence, and escalation paths.

## The Institutional Effectiveness Lesson

For institutional effectiveness professionals, the Vision Matrix offers a practical lesson: our work is not only to measure institutional performance. It is to help design the learning system that improves performance.

That means helping the college clarify:

- What outcome are we trying to move?
- What institutional behavior or student experience must change?
- What action will create that change?
- What early evidence will tell us whether we are on track?
- Who owns the next step?
- What barrier needs leadership attention?
- What will we stop, start, or adjust based on what we learn?

When IE functions this way, evidence becomes part of institutional strategy rather than an after-the-fact report.

## From Compliance to Impact

The most important shift was cultural. The Vision Matrix reminded the college that strategy is not about compliance. It is about impact.

Compliance asks, “Did we complete the task?”

Impact asks, “Did anything meaningful change for students?”

Both questions matter. But only one moves the institution forward.

> The Matrix made the work visible. The cadence made the work move. Evidence helped the work learn. Architectural boldness made the work possible.

## What Comes Next

The Vision Matrix is not just a planning tool. It is a way of working.

Its deeper lesson is that colleges do not move from vision to action by publishing better plans. They move by building better architecture: clearer outcomes, stronger ownership, faster learning cycles, better evidence, and more honest conversations about barriers.

The future of institutional effectiveness depends on helping colleges build that architecture.
